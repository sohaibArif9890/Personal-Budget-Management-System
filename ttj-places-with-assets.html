Tokyo
Akasaka (赤坂)
Neighborhood Business District Fine Dining Cultural Minato Ward
★★★★★ 4.7 (1,240 reviews) · Tourist Attraction · Neighborhood · Cultural Area

Akasaka is the upscale business and dining district of central Tokyo, sitting between the political corridors of Nagatacho and the nightlife hub of Roppongi in Minato Ward. The name means "red slope" in Japanese, a nod to the hilly streets that run through the area. During the Edo period, this was feudal lord territory — grand residences, high-ranking samurai, and an air of old importance that never really left.

Walk through Akasaka on a weekday afternoon and you'll find suits, embassies, and serious business. Come back at 8 pm, and the same streets are full of after-work crowds spilling out of izakayas, with jazz drifting up from basement bars. Weekends bring a quieter, more residential side — a relaxed place to explore shrines and walk the hilly backstreets.

Frequently Asked Questions
Everything you need to know about visiting Akasaka
Why is Akasaka famous? ⌄
Akasaka is famous for its concentration of serious Japanese restaurants — particularly kaiseki multi-course dining and traditional ryotei restaurants that have been serving politicians and foreign dignitaries for decades. It is also well known for its jazz bar scene and proximity to Akasaka Palace.
How is Akasaka different from Roppongi? ⌄
Akasaka is calmer, more business-oriented by day, and leans toward dining and jazz bars at night. Roppongi is louder, more tourist-heavy, and built around nightclubs and large art venues. The two border each other, so combining them in one evening is easy.
When is the best time to visit Akasaka? ⌄
Weekday evenings are the best time for food and atmosphere. Late March to early April is ideal for cherry blossoms around Hinokicho Park and Hikawa Shrine — far less crowded than Ueno or Shinjuku Gyoen. Weekends suit those who prefer a quieter, more relaxed pace.
Is English spoken in Akasaka? ⌄
Yes, more than most Tokyo neighborhoods. Many restaurants near the main stations carry English menus, and hotel staff are generally comfortable with English due to the strong international presence from embassies and multinational businesses.
Where should you stay in Akasaka? ⌄
The New Otani and ANA InterContinental Tokyo are the area's best-known properties. Mid-range and business hotels cluster around Akasaka-mitsuke Station for more affordable options.
